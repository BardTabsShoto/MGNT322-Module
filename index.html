2Why Evaluate Training?

Training costs time and money, so organisations need to know it works. Evaluation answers one question: did this training make the difference we intended? Without it, L&D is guessing — repeating what people enjoyed rather than what changed performance. Industry estimates put the average cost at roughly $165 per learning hour, so weak evaluation is expensive.

Two kinds of evaluation

TypeWhenPurpose
FormativeWhile you design & pilotImprove the training before/while it runs (fix confusing parts).
SummativeAfter deliveryJudge the overall effectiveness and value once it's finished.
Good practice uses both: formative to build it right, summative to prove it worked.

3The Kirkpatrick Model

Watch this 90-second explainer, then review the table below.

Kirkpatrick's model (1959, updated as the New World Kirkpatrick Model) is the most widely used framework for evaluating training. It works on four rising levels:

LevelNameQuestion it answersTypical evidence
1ReactionDid learners find it engaging, relevant, useful?Feedback survey, rating, post-session poll
2LearningDid they gain knowledge, skills, confidence?Pre/post tests, quizzes, demonstrations
3BehaviourAre they applying it on the job?Manager observation, follow-up survey weeks later
4ResultsDid it improve organisational outcomes?Business KPIs: quality, productivity, retention, cost
  • The levels build on each other — a good reaction doesn't guarantee learning, and learning doesn't guarantee changed behaviour.
  • Most organisations only measure Level 1 because it's cheapest. The real value sits at Levels 3–4.

Worked example

A bank trains tellers on a new fraud-check procedure. L1: they rate it 4.5/5. L2: a post-quiz shows 90% spot the red flags (up from 55%). L3: four weeks later managers see tellers running the checks. L4: fraud losses fall 20% that quarter. Only at L4 can the bank say the training paid off.

Going further: ROI (Phillips' Level 5)

Jack Phillips added a fifth level that turns Level-4 results into money and compares them to cost:

ROI (%) = (Net program benefits ÷ Program costs) × 100

If the training cost $10,000 and saved $30,000, net benefit = $20,000 → ROI = 200%. Powerful, but harder to isolate from other factors.

4Choosing Your Evaluation Method

The skill is matching the method to the level — and to what's realistic to measure.

LevelBest methods / toolsWatch out for
1 ReactionShort online survey, rating scale, exit pollHappy ≠ effective; keep it short
2 LearningPre/post test, quiz, skill demoTest the outcome, not trivia
3 BehaviourFollow-up survey (4–8 wks), manager checkNeeds time & manager support
4 ResultsBusiness KPIs, before/after metrics, control groupHard to isolate training's effect
Rule of thumb: pick the highest level you can realistically measure with the time and access you have — and always state your assumptions.
